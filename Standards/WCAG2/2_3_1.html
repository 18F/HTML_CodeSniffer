Success Criterion 2.3.1: Three Flashes or Below Threshold (Level A)

Web pages do not contain anything that flashes more than three times in any one second period, or the flash is below the general flash and red flash thresholds.

Note: Since any content that does not meet this success criterion can interfere with a user's ability to use the whole page, all content on the Web page (whether or not it is used to meet other success criteria) must meet this success criterion. See Conformance Requirement 5: Non-Interference.

(From Understanding WCAG 2.0: Understanding Success Criterion 2.3.1)

Note

Superseded by Success Criterion 2.3.2 at level AAA. That Success Criterion removes the flash threshold allowances.

The notice about "sufficiently small" size is deliberately vague, since it depends on whether it is for general web usage, or where the screen size is known (such as for display in a foyer). In the former case, a baseline is given based on a minimum resolution and screen size. See technique G176: Keeping the flashing area small enough for a further discussion.

Discussion

About the flash thresholds

The definitions of a "general flash" or a "red flash" are found in the "Understanding" document.

This Success Criterion can be passed by letting no part of the page flash more than 3 times per second, or ensuring that the combined area of flashing on the screen is "small enough" - roughly a 25% proportion of any 10-degree field of vision.

What is "small enough"? It depends on the size of the screen and the distance from the typical viewer. For general web usage, the W3C guidelines specify an area based on a 15-17 inch monitor at 1024 x 768 resolution, viewed at 22-26 inches (55-65 cm). This provides a (total) flashable region of 21,824 square pixels, of any shape. At higher resolutions and screen sizes, this size would cover a lesser percentage of such field of vision.

The resolution of 1024 x 768 was chosen as the "most common" resolution (when the technique was introduced with the WCAG 2.0 Recommendation, in late 2008). The techniques may be updated down the track to specify a new minimum, when a new screen size and resolution has been common for a few years. However, this is not expected to come quickly, as the main point of such a low baseline is because many users will not upgrade their computer and/or monitor any more than once every few years, so lower screen sizes need to be catered for. If the 3 flash per second limit is adhered to, then this is not an issue to worry about.

When a screen size and resolution is known (such as for use in a kiosk, or other standard operating environment), a more specific maximum flash area can be calculated. The formula can be found in the "small enough" technique (G176) above.

This replaces a stricter no-flash or "no-flicker" requirement in WCAG 1.0 (Checkpoint 7.1) which applied at single-A level. This requirement still applies at AAA level as Success Criterion 2.3.2.
